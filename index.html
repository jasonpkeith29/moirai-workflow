Platform Story
Strategic Goal And North Star

ADW is a critical enterprise data foundation. Our goal is to build a more hardened, structured, and visible platform that allows analyst teams to create governed data models and push them into Snowflake in a scalable, trusted, and repeatable way.

ADW is more than a warehouse or reporting destination. It supports trusted analytics, executive reporting, downstream decisions, and future AI use cases. ADW is the starting use case for this effort, but the broader north star is a reusable platform pattern for governed data-model creation, validation, orchestration, governance, and publication over time. The goal is to move from a model that depends heavily on manual coordination, expert knowledge, and fragmented workflow patterns to one where analysts can bring business intent and data logic, and the platform handles the repeatable mechanics. This page explains the current state, what already exists, and the platform direction required to get there.

A Shared Ingestion And Governance Platform
The next step is to turn isolated workflow improvements into a reusable platform for ingestion, validation, publication, observability, and governance so enterprise data is easier to add, trust, reuse, and maintain over time.
Story point: the answer is platform design, validation, and hardening.

The strategic issue is not that ADW is broken. It is that a critical enterprise data foundation is still being expanded and governed through a model that depends too heavily on people instead of platform behavior.

That matters because ADW is shared infrastructure for trusted analytics, executive reporting, downstream consumption, and future AI use cases. If the operating model for adding and governing data does not scale, the company will keep paying for it in slower delivery, weaker reuse, and higher governance overhead.

What the current model looks like in practice

The workflow below is trustworthy, but it creates trust through heavy manual effort. These four problem areas are the real story: too many handoffs, too much human review, too much artifact assembly, and too much governance catch-up.

Too Many Handoffs
Work moves through too many people, approvals, and coordination steps before it becomes a trusted asset.
Too Much Human Review
Analysts and ADW owners spend too much time catching issues that should be detected or guided by the platform earlier.
Too Much Artifact Assembly
DDL, metadata, views, configs, and workflow mechanics are still assembled manually too often for standard work.
Too Much Governance Catch-Up
Metadata, catalog state, and trust signals are updated after the fact instead of being enforced as part of the workflow itself.

Why it is not fit for purpose

  • The workflow produces trusted output, but it relies on human coordination at nearly every stage rather than reusable platform behavior.
  • Too many steps depend on knowing the process, the standards, the reviewers, and the right implementation pattern before work can move forward cleanly.
  • That makes the process hard to scale, hard to onboard into, and expensive to repeat for every new dataset.

What problems does it create?

  • Analysts spend too much time preparing design artifacts, finding the right standards, and iterating through review cycles before work is ready.
  • ADW owners and model reviewers absorb a heavy burden of manual quality, metadata, and semantic review.
  • Engineering and platform teams repeat too much one-off build and deployment work instead of relying on reusable patterns.
  • Current DAG SDLC steps also create friction: users navigate sync and merge overhead, unclear checkpoints, and merge or incremental load patterns that require too much manual expertise.

What has the analyst team already built?

  • ADW Process Assist and Knowledge Cards to reduce knowledge gaps and improve design-review readiness.
  • Sigma Design Form and artifact-generation tooling to reduce manual DDL, view, and metadata assembly work.
  • Deployment DAG safeguards to make controlled movement toward production less dependent on fully manual execution.

How the pain breaks down across the organization

The problem is broader than analyst friction. Product, data, and analytics each experience the current operating model differently, but they all feel the cost of fragmented workflows, weak observability, tribal standards, and low reuse.

Product
How Product Feels The Pain
Product teams depend on timely, trusted data to support roadmap decisions, customer insights, and feature measurement. The current process slows that feedback loop down.
  • New datasets and changes take too long to move from request to usable asset.
  • Trust and readiness are not always easy to interpret outside the expert team.
  • Slow data delivery makes it harder to iterate on decisions and prove value quickly.
Data Platform / Governance
How Data Feels The Pain
Data and platform teams absorb the operational and governance cost of a workflow that relies too much on manual review, disconnected controls, and locally understood standards.
  • Observability is fragmented across people, tickets, Confluence, metadata tables, and workflow tooling.
  • Airflow patterns are too manually assembled and inconsistent for a platform that needs to scale cleanly.
  • Key standards and semantic expectations live too much in tribal knowledge instead of enforceable platform behavior.
Analytics
How Analytics Feels The Pain
Analytics teams experience the workflow most directly through review loops, artifact preparation, and repeated process-navigation work before they can deliver usable data.
  • Analysts spend too much time preparing artifacts and finding the right standards before work is review-ready.
  • Quality and metadata expectations are strong, but often enforced late and manually.
  • The same repetitive mechanics are recreated too often for standard dataset work.

Limited Observability

It is too hard to see workflow health, failure modes, SLA risk, metadata completeness, and quality status in one place. Too much context lives in people, tickets, and disconnected systems.

Inefficient Airflow Patterns

Airflow is often used as a manually assembled coordination layer rather than a lightweight, standardized orchestrator. That creates operational variance and brittle workflow patterns over time.

Tribal Data Standards

Key standards around modeling, metadata, and trusted publication are understood most deeply by the analyst team and ADW insiders, rather than being encoded into a shared platform.

Slow Delivery

When every new dataset requires heavy process navigation, review preparation, and custom assembly, product and analytics work takes longer to move from idea to usable asset.

Governance That Does Not Scale Easily

Governance exists, but too much of it is enforced after the fact through meetings, signoffs, and manual updates instead of system-level controls that scale across teams.

Low Reusability Across Domains

Without a shared platform pattern, each domain risks solving similar workflow, quality, and publication problems independently instead of building on a common foundation.

Why the current response is still not enough

The current automation work is meaningful, but it does not yet answer the broader platform questions on its own. We have not fully aligned on the target operating model, the governance boundaries, the semantic-model controls, the publication rules, or the platform capabilities that should be shared across ADW and other future datasets.

  • Some current work should likely be retained and hardened.
  • Some current work needs validation before it can be trusted at scale.
  • Some important platform capabilities still need to be designed and built net new.

What we need to solve now

  • Align on a platform structure that reduces analyst friction without weakening trust, metadata discipline, or production governance.
  • Evaluate the analyst team’s current automation work in the context of a broader shared platform, not as isolated point solutions.
  • Define the missing capabilities needed to make the foundation reusable for ADW first and other governed datasets later.

The proposal is not to replace governance with AI or to automate one narrow workflow in isolation. The proposal is to build a shared platform that supports different user types, standardizes the repeated mechanics, and preserves explicit governance where trust matters most.

What the proposed technical solution actually includes, and why it matters

In practical terms, this means structured submission paths tailored to different personas, validation and guided remediation before work becomes implementation effort, pattern-based generation of workflows and artifacts, controlled orchestration of Snowflake, EKS, dbt, and related compute, and publication controls that distinguish exploratory assets from certified enterprise assets. Just as importantly, the goal is to reduce or eliminate the amount of manual DAG construction and SDLC navigation that standard analysts and builders have to do themselves. This matters because a one-time ADW workflow improvement would reduce some pain, but it would not solve the broader organizational problem. A platform approach creates shared standards, repeatable quality controls, clearer trust signaling, and lower operational variance every time a new dataset is introduced.

Execution Plan

Move From Strategy To A Controlled Platform Pilot

The goal is not to declare the future-state design finished. The goal is to validate the strongest current pieces, close the most important governance and quality gaps, and move into a controlled pilot that proves the platform can work in practice.

What this phase is really about

This is the point where the organization turns a strong story into a practical plan. The near-term work should focus on validating what already exists, hardening what matters most, and proving the platform pattern in a small but meaningful way before it expands.

Recommended rollout path

The safest and most credible path forward is staged. Each step builds confidence before the next one expands scope, trust, or technical responsibility.

1
First Step

Validate What Already Exists

  • Pressure-test ADW Process Assist, Knowledge Cards, artifact generation, and deployment DAG behavior against real analyst and modeler use cases.
  • Measure reduction in review loops, analyst time, and manual implementation work.
Focus: prove the current components are useful enough to keep and build on.
2
Control Layer

Harden Governance and Trust Controls

  • Define approval boundaries, publish gates, metadata completeness rules, and Lane A versus Lane B trust signaling.
  • Clarify what remains deterministic and what is AI-assisted.
Focus: make sure faster workflow does not weaken trust, policy, or publication controls.
3
Pilot Stage

Pilot the Platform Pattern

  • Start with a scoped set of standard ADW asset types where the benefit is high and governance risk is manageable.
  • Run the new workflow alongside the old one until success criteria are met.
Focus: prove the workflow works in the real world before wider rollout.
4
Scale Out

Expand Beyond The First Use Case

  • Reuse the same platform foundation for additional governed datasets, publication paths, and domain-owned data products.
  • Avoid creating a one-off Product Analytics solution that cannot scale organizationally.
Focus: turn a successful pilot into a reusable enterprise capability.

Workflow in practice: what happens, where it happens, and what runs it

This is the ideal end-to-end platform flow. Each stage shows the user surface, the underlying system of work, the likely technology choice, and the main technical question still to resolve.

Stage 1

Request and Intake

The analyst starts the process in a chat-style interface by describing the business need, the domain context, and the kind of ADW asset they are trying to create. They should also be able to paste a starting SQL query or logic pattern so the system can use that as input rather than forcing them to begin from scratch.

Where the user is

In an LLM-driven chat interface, ideally in a platform they already use regularly. A Snowflake-native starting point, potentially using Cortex, is a plausible first surface if that proves usable for analysts.

What system does the work

The chat layer captures the request, asks clarifying follow-up questions, summarizes the analyst's intent, and turns the conversation and any starter SQL into a structured request contract the platform can validate and route.

LLM Chat Interface Starter SQL Input Request Contract
Open design decision: whether the first POC should start inside Snowflake using Cortex as the analyst-facing chat surface, or use a separate lightweight web interface that can later integrate more tightly with Snowflake.
Stage 2

Validation and Routing

The platform now needs to determine whether the request makes sense, whether the starter SQL or logic is efficient enough to build from, and whether the analyst is asking for a new asset, a change to an existing asset, or something that may already exist in ADW.

Where it happens

In the backend platform service layer immediately after intake, before anything is generated for Snowflake, dbt, or Airflow.

What system does the work

A validation and routing service checks the business intent, looks for missing information, reviews the starter SQL for obvious inefficiencies or anti-patterns, and compares the request against known assets or existing patterns before choosing the next path.

Business Need Validation SQL Review Existing Asset Check
Open design decision: how much of the SQL review and duplicate-asset detection should be deterministic versus LLM-assisted, and how confidently the POC can identify when an existing ADW asset should be reused instead of creating something new.
Stage 3

Asset Design and Artifact Generation

Once the request is validated, the platform translates the analyst's business intent, starter SQL, and routing decision into a durable ADW asset pattern. This is where exploratory logic starts becoming a reusable fact table, dimension, metric layer, or change to an existing governed asset.

Where the user is

The analyst is still in the platform workflow reviewing the recommended asset shape and any generated outputs, while the system handles the underlying technical assembly.

What system does the work

An asset-design and generation service refines the SQL or logic as needed, maps it to the right ADW pattern, and produces the technical outputs required for execution, testing, metadata, and orchestration.

Asset Patterning Artifact Generator Generated SQL / Config
Open design decision: how much the POC should auto-rewrite or optimize analyst SQL versus preserving it more directly and only wrapping it in a standard asset pattern.
Stage 4

Execution and Validation in Lower Environments

The generated asset pattern now runs in lower environments so the team can confirm that the output is correct, the SQL or transformation logic behaves as intended, quality checks pass, and the workflow is reliable before broader publication.

Where it happens

In the orchestration runtime and the data execution systems, with review happening through the platform and lower-environment output surfaces rather than in the intake flow itself.

What system does the work

Airflow orchestrates the run, Snowflake performs the data work, dbt can be used where transformation logic benefits from it, and the platform surfaces the output back for review so the analyst and broader team can validate that the asset is actually usable.

Airflow Snowflake Analyst Output Review
Open design decision: how much validation the analyst should do directly in the platform versus in familiar lower-environment data tools, and which asset types should stay Snowflake-native versus use dbt in the POC.
Stage 5

Approval, Governance, and Publish

Before the output is published more broadly, the platform checks that the agreed controls have been met. This is where trust, metadata completeness, approval state, and the chosen publication path come together.

Where it happens

In the publish-control layer, governance checkpoints, and metadata or catalog surfaces connected to the ADW asset.

What system does the work

The platform enforces deterministic publish rules, writes trust and metadata state back to the right systems, and leaves only the approvals that genuinely need a human decision.

Publish Gate Metadata Layer Catalog / Trust Update
Open design decision: which governance checks can be fully automated in the POC and which still require explicit human sign-off.
Stage 6

Support, Monitoring, and Recovery

Once the workflow is live, it needs a support model that Engineering can actually own. That means run visibility, failures, alerting, rollback paths, and clear ownership cannot be an afterthought.

Where it happens

In the observability and support layer that sits alongside Airflow, Snowflake, and the platform services.

What system does the work

Monitoring, run history, alerting, and recovery tooling capture what happened and make the workflow supportable as a real platform capability.

Monitoring Run Logs Rollback / Remediation
Open design decision: what the minimum observability and support model needs to be for Engineering to credibly own the POC once it is running.

Working assumptions for the ideal technical shape

These are the assumptions that make the current proposal coherent, given the technology stack the organization already has.

Working Assumption

Interaction should start in a Snowflake-native analyst surface

Most users in this workflow are analysts who already spend time in Snowflake and can often provide starter SQL. The intake experience should meet them there through a Snowflake-native, LLM-driven interface that captures intent, logic, and context without asking them to build workflow mechanics.

  • A Snowflake-native analyst interface is the preferred intake surface
  • Cortex should power the conversational and reasoning layer
  • Users should be able to provide starter SQL or logic as input
Working Assumption

Validation should combine deterministic checks with targeted AI assist

The platform should enforce the known rules through application logic first, but this stage also needs help evaluating starter SQL, spotting anti-patterns, and checking whether the request maps to an existing asset. AI assistance should strengthen that review, not replace deterministic controls.

  • Business intent, required fields, and routing logic should be deterministic
  • SQL review and duplicate-asset detection can be partly LLM-assisted
  • Governance logic should stay explainable and auditable
Working Assumption

Asset patterning and generation should be a platform service

Validated analyst logic should be translated into the right ADW asset pattern by a reusable service layer rather than manually assembled by each analyst. That service should handle SQL refinement, generated outputs, metadata updates, and the workflow artifacts needed downstream.

  • Generation logic should be pattern-driven and reusable
  • Starter SQL can be refined, wrapped, or optimized by the platform
  • Config should be created and managed by the platform where possible
Working Assumption

Airflow and Snowflake remain the core execution layers

Snowflake should continue to handle the data work and Airflow should continue to orchestrate it, but both should sit behind cleaner platform patterns. dbt should be used where it helps, not as a mandatory layer everywhere.

  • Snowflake remains the main data execution and storage layer
  • Airflow remains the orchestration runtime
  • dbt is an intentional option, not a universal requirement
